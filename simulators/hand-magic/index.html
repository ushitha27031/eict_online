Hold one or both hands in view of the camera. Move quickly to charge sparks and ripples, open your palm wide to build a slow pulse, make a fist to trigger a burst, and pinch thumb to index finger to cast a spark. Bring both hands close together to braid their energy into one.

What's actually happening: your webcam video never leaves your computer. A small model (called MediaPipe Hands, made by Google) runs entirely inside your browser and looks at each video frame to guess the position of 21 key points on every hand it sees — the tip and joints of each finger, plus the wrist. That's the same basic idea behind sign-language tools, AR filters, and touchless gesture controls.

The lines you see are this page's own artwork, layered a few ways: the finger "bones" get a glow plus a bright core plus a little pulse of light traveling along them; a faint web connects the fingertips to each other; and a rotating ring behind the palm grows or shrinks with how open your hand is.

The effects react to motion: this page compares each hand's position frame to frame, and the faster it moves, the more sparks, ripples, and glow you get — that's the "motion energy" reading in the HUD. Fist and open-palm shapes are detected with simple distance math (how far the fingertips sit from the palm's center), which is enough to trigger their own burst effects without anything fancier than geometry.
